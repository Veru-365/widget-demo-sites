Pricing

How pricing will work

We're being upfront: partner pricing is not a published rate card yet — it's finalized together during the pilot. Here is the working model we'd start that conversation from.

Working model: a per-client-org platform fee, with volume tiers. Each advisor firm you onboard is one organization on the platform; you pay a platform fee per active org, and the per-org rate steps down as your client count grows. You set your own pricing to your clients on top — your margin is your business, and we don't participate in it.

The metering to support this already runs in the platform today: per-org usage reports (active users, conversations, AI token consumption) with CSV export, so both sides bill from the same numbers. Invoicing is handled manually today — there is no payment portal yet — which for a pilot is a feature: it means terms stay flexible while we find the structure that fits your volume.

No fabricated numbers here on purpose. Rates depend on expected client count and what's bundled per tier, and quoting a placeholder figure would only anchor the wrong conversation. Bring your expected client volume to the pricing call (see the last section) and we'll put real numbers on paper together during the pilot.

FAQ

The questions partners ask first, answered straight

Can the widget go on our website and many separate landing pages?

Yes — and rather than tell you, we can show you. The install is one script tag per page, and the same tag works on any number of pages and any number of separate domains. There is no per-page setup, ever. We built a fictional client, "Acme Wealth," with three live pages on a third-party domain — a main website, a retirement campaign page, and a tax season landing page — each embedding the same live widget. The install snippet and provisioning flow are on the overview page.

One honest detail: each page load starts a fresh chat session. Conversation context carries through a visitor's session on that page, not across different pages — that's the standard behavior for anonymous website chat.

The widget isn't a text bot: it renders live financial modules — Monte Carlo retirement projections, net-worth cards, what-if comparisons — inside the chat, expandable to full size. Try it on any of the demo pages linked above.

Who owns the client relationship — and the leads?

You do. Advisor firms are your clients; we never contact them or their investors. When a website visitor engages the widget, the built-in lead-capture card (it renders as a form for firms that have published their public advisor page) stores the lead inside that firm's own tenant, where the firm's advisor picks it up — the lead never leaves the client org.

For your systems, every org supports outbound webhooks: JSON events pushed to endpoints you register (up to 10 per org, HMAC-signed when you set a signing secret on the endpoint), with delivery audit stats. Today's event catalog covers platform events like user.created, fact_finder.completed, and branding.updated; extending it with lead events for your marketing stack is exactly the kind of item we'd scope in the pilot.

What does the advisor firm actually get beyond the widget?

The widget is the front door to a complete branded portal. Each firm gets an AI planning assistant that runs real financial tools — Monte Carlo retirement projections, what-if scenario modeling, portfolio analysis — plus personal financial statements that fill themselves from conversation or uploaded documents. Around that: a compliant document vault, appointment scheduling, and automatic activity sync into the CRMs advisors already use (Wealthbox, Salesforce, Microsoft 365, RingCentral), with per-firm feature switches so you can package tiers however you sell them.

What happens if a client firm cancels?

Offboarding is a deliberate, controlled process — one API call deactivates the org, removes all members, and revokes any live bank-data connections immediately. Records are retained rather than hard-deleted, because in this industry a regulator can ask for them years later; that retention posture is a feature, not a bug. What a departing firm can take with them (exports of their data) is something we'd define together in pilot terms — usage and billing data already exports to CSV today, and we'd scope broader export needs with you rather than overpromise here.

What's the support model?

During the pilot, honest answer: you get the founding team directly — a shared channel, weekly scheduled check-ins, and same-day responses on anything blocking a client launch. We are an early-stage platform and we treat pilot partners accordingly: your issues are our roadmap. Formal SLAs and a tiered support structure are part of the post-pilot commercial agreement, sized to your rollout.

Where do the AI's answers come from?

The assistant is a large-language-model application, but the numbers it shows are not model guesses: when it presents a retirement projection or a net-worth figure, it is calling the platform's own calculation engines — the same Monte Carlo simulation and financial-statement data that power the full portal — and rendering the result as an interactive module in the chat. The assistant is built to hand off to the firm's human advisor rather than act as one, and conversations are retained on the platform — nothing is ephemeral. Like any AI system it isn't infallible, which is why the product's posture is "assistant plus human advisor," not "replace the advisor."
